What is administrative burden?


Administrative burden is the costs associated with applying for, receiving, and participating in government benefits and services [1]. They can appear in many ways that can impede individuals' ability to easily access assistance. A complex application process requires individuals to know how to navigate the application processes for several programs, which can be intricate and overwhelming. Documentation requirements force individuals to dedicate time to collect documents, which may not be easily accessible.


Additionally, renewal requirements make previously approved applicants repeatedly demonstrate their ongoing eligibility, an unnecessary step if their circumstances have not changed. Some services necessitate an in-person application process, demanding individuals take extra time out of their day for something that could potentially be more conveniently completed online.

Why do we care about administrative burden?


Administrative burden can reduce the enrollment in essential services, leading to underutilization of social support programs. In addition to hurting individuals who might benefit directly from social services, administrative burden can have broader societal effects:


A positive externality is when private demand and supply curves do not accurately measure marginal social benefits. More simply put, it is when there are positive side effects of a program that are not included in the program's price tag.

  • For Medicaid, access to healthcare for an individual can reduce the spread of sickness for the community.
  • SNAP has been shown to reduce healthcare costs [2].
  • SNAP has been shown to benefit the economy at large. In a weak economy, $1 in SNAP benefits generates $1.50 in economic activity [3].

A negative externality is when private demand and supply curves do not accurately measure marginal social cost. More simply put, it is when negative side effects are not included in the costs of the program.

  • For Medicaid, uninsured individuals with uncompensated care pass costs to other users of the medical system [4].
  • For TANF, every $1 less to families is associated with an $8 cost to society due to an increase in spending for children and parents' health [5].

What are the benefits of social support programs?


Redistribution involves the transfer of resources from one group to another and is often implemented through government intervention such as social support programs, which are designed to shift resources from high-income individuals to those with lower incomes [6]. This process can help reduce economic inequality and maximize social welfare.


The concept of consumption smoothing relates to the redistribution of consumption from periods of high consumption, where marginal utility is low, to periods when consumption is less and marginal utility is higher [7]. This is the idea that individuals have a preference for consistent levels of consumption (spending) over time, avoiding fluctuations between extremes that can disrupt an individual's standard of living. Programs such as Medicaid, TANF, and SNAP are designed with a consumption smoothing model in mind, particularly for low-income individuals. For instance, a low-income individual with access to Medicaid is able to receive healthcare services on a regular basis, stabilizing their healthcare consumption. Without such support, the individual would likely face periods of reduced healthcare consumption, deviating from the ideal of consumption smoothing.
