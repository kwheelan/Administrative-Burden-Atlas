What is administrative burden?


Administrative burden is the costs associated with applying for, receiving, and participating in government benefits and services [1]. They can appear in many ways that can impede individuals' ability to easily access assistance. A complex application process requires individuals to know how to navigate through the application processes for several programs, which can be intricate and overwhelming. Documentation requirements force individuals to dedicate time to collect documents, which may not be easily accessible.


Additionally, renewal requirements make previously approved applicants repeatedly demonstrate their ongoing eligibility, an unnecessary step if their circumstances have not changed. Some services necessitate an in-person application process, demanding individuals take extra time out of their day for something that could potentially be more conveniently completed online. These examples demonstrate the complex process of receiving essential social services.

Why do we care about administrative burden?


Administrative burden can reduce the enrollment in essential services, leading to underutilization of social support programs. In addition to hurting individuals who might benefit directly from social services, administrative burden can have broader societal effects:


A positive externality is when private demand and supply curves do not accurately measure marginal social benefits. More simply put, it is when there are positive side effects of a program that are not included in the program's price tag.

  • For Medicaid, access to healthcare for an individual can reduce the spread of sickness for the community.
  • SNAP has been shown to reduce healthcare costs [2].
  • SNAP has been shown to benefit the economy at large. In a weak economy, $1 in SNAP benefits generates $1.50 in economic activity [3].

A negative externality is when private demand and supply curves do not accurately measure marginal social cost. More simply put, when negative side effects are not included in the costs of the program.

  • For Medicaid, uninsured individuals with uncompensated care pass costs to other users of the medical system [4].
  • For TANF, every $1 less to families is associated with an $8 cost to society due to an increase in spending for children and parents' health [5].

What are the benefits of social support programs?


Redistribution involves the transfer of resources from one group to another and is often implemented through government intervention such as social support programs, which are designed to shift resources from high-income individuals to those with lower incomes [6]. This process has been recognized for its positive impacts in reducing economic inequality and for fostering policies that prioritize the maximization of social welfare.


The concept of consumption smoothing relates to the redistribution of consumption from periods of high consumption, where marginal utility is low, to periods when consumption is less and marginal utility is higher [7]. This is the idea that individuals have a preference for consistent levels of consumption over time, avoiding fluctuations between extremes that can disrupt an individual's standard of living. Programs such as Medicaid, TANF, and SNAP are designed with a consumption smoothing model in mind, particularly for low-income individuals. For instance, a low-income individual with access to Medicaid is able to receive healthcare services on a regular basis, stabilizing their healthcare consumption. Without such support, the individual would likely face periods of reduced healthcare consumption, deviating from the ideal of consumption smoothing.

About Medicaid

×

Medicaid is a health care program that is federally mandated but state run for low-income families and low-income disabled and elderly [1]. Eligibility is determined based on income and family structure. The program is federally funded through matching grants, and there are federal mandates for minimum eligibility and service coverage. States are permitted to administer their programs to increase eligibility and increase coverage, so there is a wide variation in Medicaid design [2]. Medicaid is the single largest source of health coverage in the United States with 77.9 million individuals covered [3].

Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF)

×

TANF is a cash welfare program that is federally funded but state run for low-income families with children [1]. Eligibility is determined by income thresholds set by each state [2]. The program is jointly funded through federal block grants and state maintenance of effort contributions. There are federal mandates for time restrictions and work requirements. States are permitted to design their own programs, so there is a wide variation in what type of support and amount of support provided. An estimated 2.8 million people receive assistance in 2023 [3].

Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP)

×

SNAP is an in-kind program that provides nutritional support for low-income individuals. Eligibility is determined by state cash welfare programs like TANF or by monthly cash income [1]. The program is federally funded but administered by the state [2]. The federal government sets eligibility limits and benefit levels, so there is little variation between states. States do design the application process with federal guidelines, so there is variation in the application [3]. SNAP is the largest anti-hunger program with an estimated 38 million recipients [4].
